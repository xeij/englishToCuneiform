Here's a strange thing about Old Persian cuneiform. It was probably invented on purpose, by one specific man, for one specific reason. Darius the Great commissioned a brand-new writing system around 525 BCE. He wanted to carve his life story onto a cliff face in a script no one else owned. He basically says as much in the inscription itself.

It borrows its wedges from the older Mesopotamian cuneiform. Those were terrifying systems with hundreds of signs that took scribes a decade to master. Next to them, Old Persian is small and almost embarrassingly neat. Thirty-six phonetic signs. Eight logograms for words the king used a lot: 𐏈 Auramazdā, 𐏋 king, 𐏌 country, 𐏏 earth. A handful of numerals. A tiny vertical wedge 𐏐 between words. You could learn the whole inventory in an afternoon.

And almost no one did. Outside royal monuments, hardly anyone wrote anything in it. The empire ran its day-to-day paperwork in Aramaic. Old Persian was for shouting at history.

If you're ever in western Iran on the old road from Babylon to Ecbatana, look up. About a hundred metres above the highway, on a sheer cliff no enemy could easily reach, Darius had his entire origin story carved in three languages: Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian. The relief at the top shows him with his foot on a defeated rebel. The text underneath lists every battle of the year he took the throne. Nineteen of them, by his count.

For two and a half thousand years that inscription sat up there, unreadable. Then in the 1830s a young British officer named Henry Rawlinson decided he was going to copy it. He climbed the cliff with ladders and ropes, sometimes dangling from a single hand, dictating signs to an assistant. Reading those signs eventually unlocked all of Mesopotamian writing. The trick was using them to crack the older Babylonian half, which then opened up everything else. Three thousand years of literature came back online because one man refused to be sensible about a cliff.

The Achaemenid Persian Empire was, for a couple of centuries, simply the biggest thing humans had ever built. At its peak it ran from the Indus to the Aegean and from the Caucasus to the deserts of Libya. Around fifty million people lived under it. That was somewhere between a third and a half of everyone alive on the planet at the time.

What's strange is how lightly it sat on them. The Persians taxed you. They wanted troops when there was a war on. They expected you to call their king King of Kings. Past that they mostly left you alone. They didn't care which gods you worshipped. They didn't try to make you speak Persian. When Cyrus took Babylon he restored the cults the previous king had suppressed. When Cambyses took Egypt he had himself crowned as pharaoh and made offerings to Egyptian gods. It was an empire of light-touch governance. That was partly out of principle. It was also partly because nothing heavier-handed would have held that much territory together.

The plumbing of it was famous. Twenty provinces, each run by an appointed governor. A 2,700-kilometre paved road from Sardis on the Aegean to Susa in the heartland, fast enough that Herodotus claimed a royal messenger could ride it in a week. Uniform silver and gold coinage. The world's first real postal system.

𐎤𐎢𐎽𐎢𐏁 Cyrus the Great is the one even the Greeks couldn't bring themselves to insult. He started out as a minor princeling under the Median king Astyages, who was reportedly his own grandfather. Within about twenty years he had taken Media, Lydia (rich Croesus and all), and Babylon. He took Babylon without a fight. The gates opened for him. The first thing he did was release the Jewish exiles his predecessors had dragged there and let them go rebuild their temple. His Cylinder, dug up in 1879, is sometimes called the world's first human-rights document. It's a stretch, but only a small one. He died chasing nomads in Central Asia, and nobody quite knows where.

His son 𐎣𐎲𐎢𐎪𐎡𐎹 Cambyses II is mostly famous for two things. The first is conquering Egypt, which was an enormous achievement. The second is whatever happened to him on the road home, which was apparently very bad. Herodotus says he went mad. He died, possibly by his own hand, while a usurper named Gaumāta seized the throne back in Persia.

𐎭𐎠𐎼𐎹𐎺𐎢𐏁 Darius the Great is the one everything comes back to. He wasn't the obvious heir. By most accounts he was a distant cousin, a spear-bearer in the royal guard. Inside a year of taking power he had crushed nine revolts and stabilized a wobbling empire. Then he set about organizing it. He invented the satrapy system. He standardized the coinage. He built the road network and a canal from the Nile to the Red Sea. He founded the new capital at Persepolis. He commissioned the Behistun inscription. He's the ancient world's great administrator. He's also the reason you can read any of this.

His son 𐎧𐏁𐎹𐎠𐎼𐏁𐎠 Xerxes inherited the empire at its peak. He is mostly remembered for failing to add Greece to it. His 480 BCE invasion is the Thermopylae and Salamis story. He forced the pass against Leonidas and his three hundred Spartans. He burned an evacuated Athens. Then he lost his fleet in the straits of Salamis and went home. He kept building at Persepolis. He was eventually murdered in a palace coup. He is also the same Xerxes who shows up in the Book of Esther as Ahasuerus.

𐎠𐎼𐎫𐎧𐏁𐏂𐎠 Artaxerxes I made peace with Athens and ended the wars his father had started. He let Ezra return to Jerusalem and sent Nehemiah back to rebuild its walls. A quieter reign, which after Xerxes counts as a virtue.

Darius III had the spectacular bad luck to be on the throne when Alexander the Great arrived. He lost to him twice, first at Issus and then at Gaugamela. He was murdered in flight by one of his own satraps. Alexander burned Persepolis the same year. According to one account, he did it drunk at a banquet, as revenge for Xerxes' burning of Athens a century and a half before.

Persepolis itself was the imperial showpiece. Darius started it around 518 BCE on a great stone terrace under a mountain the Persians called the Mountain of Mercy. It wasn't really a working capital. Susa and Babylon did the day-to-day governing. But every spring at Nowruz, the Persian new year, ambassadors from every corner of the empire would climb the Apadana stairs in a long procession, each carrying tribute. Horses from Cappadocia. Lapis from Bactria. Gold dust from India. The processions are still there, carved in low relief on the walls. Alexander's troops looted the treasury and burned the palaces in 330 BCE. The columns and reliefs that remain are some of the most powerful things anyone has ever left behind in stone.

Read enough Old Persian inscriptions and two words come back again and again. The first is 𐎠𐎼𐎫 arta, which means truth, justice, and right order. The second is 𐎭𐎼𐎢𐎥 drauga, the Lie. The kings worshipped Auramazda, the wise lord of the prophet Zoroaster, and saw themselves as his agents in a cosmic argument between the two. Every revolt against the throne was a small victory for the Lie. Every grain shipment that got where it was supposed to go was a small triumph for the Truth. It's a strikingly ethical way to think about administration. Darius closes the Behistun text by warning whoever reads it: do not leave the right path. Do not act in defiance. Whoever does, may Auramazda strike him. He meant it.

Old Persian itself is Indo-European. It is a cousin of Sanskrit and the great-great-grandparent of the Persian people still speak today. After Alexander, the spoken language went on. The cuneiform did not. Through the Parthian and Sasanian centuries the speech evolved into Middle Persian. After the Arab conquests it took on a modified Arabic script. The thread is unbroken. If you can read a Tehran newspaper, you are reading something very distantly descended from the language of Darius.

The script itself sat unread for the better part of two thousand years. Carsten Niebuhr made the first accurate copies in the 1760s. A young German Latin teacher named Grotefend guessed the names of Darius and Xerxes from those copies in 1802, working it out essentially as a puzzle in his spare time. Rawlinson, hanging off the cliff at Behistun, did the rest.

These are exactly the same wedges the masons of Darius and Xerxes cut into Persian limestone two and a half thousand years ago. We didn't redesign them. We didn't tidy them up. The shapes have not changed.

A small thing that helps when you start reading these texts. Almost every paragraph opens with the same three signs: 𐎰𐎠𐎫𐎡𐎹 θātiy, "he says." Then the king's name. Then the title. Behistun, the longest of them, repeats the formula seventy-six times. It is not laziness. It is the voice of a king dictated through a chisel — every paragraph is a sentence the king is literally saying to the cliff face. Two and a half thousand years later you can still hear him beginning to speak.

Scholars catalogue the inscriptions with three-letter codes. The first letter is the king — D for Darius, X for Xerxes, A for Artaxerxes, C for Cyrus. The second is the site — B Behistun, P Persepolis, N Naqsh-e Rustam, S Susa, H Hamadan, E Elvend, V Van, Z Suez. The third is just a sequence letter. So DB is Darius at Behistun. DNa is Darius at Naqsh-e Rustam, text A — his tomb. XPh is Xerxes at Persepolis, text H — the famous "daiva inscription" where Xerxes brags about smashing some other people's idols. Once you have the codes, the whole corpus opens up. There are about two hundred of them.

On the tomb at Naqsh-e Rustam, Darius lists every country he ruled, in order, like a roll call. Twenty-three of them. Persia, Elam, Babylonia, Assyria, Arabia, Egypt, Lydia, Ionia, Media, Armenia, Cappadocia, Parthia, Drangiana, Aria, Chorasmia, Bactria, Sogdia, Gandhara, Scythia, Sattagydia, Arachosia, Maka, India. Above the list, in relief, thirty representatives of those peoples hold up his throne with their bare hands. The implication is exactly what it looks like.

The script itself is a thing of strange design. It has thirty-six phonetic signs, but it isn't a true alphabet and it isn't a normal syllabary either. Most consonants have one sign that carries an inherent a. Nine consonants — k, g, t, d, m, n, r, v, j — have an extra sign for the same consonant with i or u. So you can write ka, ki, ku as separate signs, but only pa for any p-syllable. Why some consonants and not others? Nobody really knows. It looks like a writing system designed in a single sitting by someone making it up as they went.

The Cyrus Cylinder, dug up in the rubble of Babylon in 1879, is not in Old Persian at all — it's in Akkadian cuneiform, addressed to Cyrus's new Babylonian subjects in their own language. It opens with the standard Babylonian cursing of the previous king as a tyrant who neglected the gods, then announces Cyrus as their rescuer, sent by Marduk. Then it does something that has no real parallel in the ancient near east: it says he returned the displaced peoples to their homes and let them rebuild their temples. The Hebrew Bible records the same policy from the other side of the transaction. The cylinder is now in the British Museum. A replica sits at the United Nations.

Two words show up on the Persepolis terrace inscriptions with surprising regularity: 𐏁𐎡𐎹𐎠𐎫𐎡𐏁 šiyātiš, "happiness," and 𐎢𐎱𐎿𐎫𐎠 upastā, "aid." Auramazdā creates happiness for mankind. The king asks for Auramazdā's aid in everything he builds. Read enough of them and they start to feel less like propaganda and more like a real theology of governance — the king as a craftsman, the empire as a project the gods are quietly helping along.
